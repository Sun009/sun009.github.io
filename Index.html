Projects

  Modeling the Memetics using Edge Diversity:

In this project, we study how does a meme spread on the network. Based on the in-depth analysis of real world datasets, we observe that the central people called core nodes play an important role to make a meme viral in the network. We further study how the memes are spread over communities and analysis shows that once the information hits the central nodes it takes no time to spread in multiple communities. We propose a spreading model based on the spreading power of different kind of edges that is directly related to their structural position in the network. The proposed methodologies are verified using the Twitter dataset of Higgs-Boson meme. The research papers related to this project are published in Asonam 2015 and Complenet 2016.

  Predict the global rank of a node using its local information:

With time, the size of complex networks is increasing very fast. It is not feasible to collect the entire network at any given time to study its characteristics. This creates the need to propose local algorithms to study various global properties of the network. In this project, we estimate the global rank of a node using its local information. In first work, we have proposed four methods to estimate degree rank of a node without having the entire network (The research papers related to this project are published in Comsnets 2016 and accepted in Asonam 2017). Next, we focus on the closeness centrality of the node that denotes the reachability of the network from the given node. We propose a fast heuristic method to estimate the closeness rank of a node based on the structural properties of real world networks. The time complexity to estimate the closeness rank of a node is same as to compute its closeness centrality (This work is accepted in Asonam 2017). We are further working to estimate global rank of the nodes based on other centrality measures like betweenness centrality, PageRank, coreness, and so on. My Ph.D. thesis is based on the proposed project.

  Social Network Analysis of the Caste-Based Reservation System in India:

Being as old as the human civilisation, discrimination based on various grounds as race, creed, gender, and caste is prevailing in the world from a long time. To undo the impact of this long enduring historical discrimination, governments worldwide have adopted various forms of affirmative action; such as positive discrimination, employment equity, and quota system. Locally known as “Reservation” policy, affirmative action in India is one of the world's oldest and most complex affirmative policy. Although being one of the most controversial and frequently debated issues, the reservation system in India lacks a rigorous scientific study and analysis. In this paper, we discuss the dynamics of the reservation system based on the cultural divide among Indian population using social network analysis. The mathematical model, using Erdos-Renyi network, shows that the addition of weak ties between the two components leads to a logarithmic reduction in the social distance. Our experimental simulations establish the claim for the different clans of frequently studied social network models as well as real world networks. We further show that a small number of links created by the process of reservation are adequate for a society to live in harmony. (This work is ready to be submitted.)

  Coreness Approximation using Local Information:

Coreness is one of the important centrality measure that tells about the influential power of a node. K-shell decomposition method is a widely used method to compute the coreness of a node. In this work, we approximate the coreness of a node using local information. Next, we use the approximated coreness value to estimate the global coreness rank of a node without having the entire network. (This project is ready to be submitted.)

  Meso-scale structures in complex networks:

This project mainly focuses on the evolution of meso-scale structures like core-periphery structure or community structure in complex networks. It is observed that real world scale-free networks possess both the community as well as the core-periphery structure that shows the modular and hierarchical organization in the given networks. First, we study how both community and core-periphery structure coexist in a network. Based on the analysis, we propose evolving models to generate synthetic weighted and unweighted scale-free networks having both meso-scale structures. This work is published in Comsnets 2016.

  Analysis of Dark Multi-layered Networks:

In this project, we present a synthesized analysis of the terrorist network datasets through the analysis of the multiple layers of these networks. These networks show different behaviors than the scale-free real world networks (The work based on this analysis is accepted in SNAA, Asonam 2017.). The main challenges associated with these networks are incompleteness, fuzzy boundaries, and dynamic behavior. We account these characteristics and propose a method to identify knowledge sharing communities (KSC) (This work was presented in Sunbelt 2016, and It is communicated in Social Networks Journal.). Next, we propose models to generate the synthetic networks based on the existing datasets (This work is accepted in SI, Asonam 2017.).
